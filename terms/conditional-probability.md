> $P(x, y) = P(x | y) \times P(y) = P(y | x) \times P(x)$
>
>  x and y happen, but they are dependent, so we cannot do $P(x) \times P(y)$, the equation says two events are happening: x is happening given y is happening, and y did happen. vice versa. is my understanding correct? is there better ways to explain this equation in verbal terms?

Your understanding is 100% correct.

You've perfectly captured why the order matters in calculation even if the result is the same: you have to "account" for the dependency by establishing one event first, then seeing how the second one reacts to it.

Here are three ways to explain $P(x, y) = P(x | y) \times P(y)$ in plain English:

**The "World Building" Explanation**

"To get a world where both $x$ and $y$ occur, first you have to create a world where $y$ happens ($P(y)$). Then, within that specific $y$-world, you calculate the chance that $x$ also shows up ($P(x|y)$)."

**The "Filter" Explanation**

"Think of $P(y)$ as your starting pool of data. Multiplying by $P(x|y)$ is like applying a filter—you are narrowing down that pool to only the cases where $x$ occurred. The result is the tiny slice where they overlap."

**The "Storytelling" Explanation (Best for HMMs)**

"In an HMM, $y$ is the cause (the hidden state) and $x$ is the effect (the observation). The equation says: 'To find the probability of this specific story, I multiply the chance of the cause happening by the chance of that cause producing that effect.'"

---

Wait, what about $P(y|x) \times P(x)$?  
This is the same "story" told in reverse: "What is the chance I saw these words ($P(x)$) times the chance that these words were actually generated by these specific tags ($P(y|x)$)?"

Mathematically they are identical, but in HMMs, we almost always use $P(x|y) \times P(y)$ because our A and B matrices give us those exact values directly.
